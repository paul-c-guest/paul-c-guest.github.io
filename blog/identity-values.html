Identity, Values, and Strengths Exploration

24 June 2023

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

I've chosen to not eat meat if I can avoid it. The reasons are ethical, and a bit ecological. Basically what it comes down to is that I don't need animals to die so I can stay alive. But what makes animals special enough that they shouldn't be dying to fill my freezer? Really the more complex standpoint is around the very small range of animals that we have chosen to farm intensively, and the crazy use of resources that requires.

The big thing that changed wasn't 'not eating meat', but becoming the vegetarian at the barbecue; the vegetarian at the restaurant. Unless that barbecue was fired up just to char some vegetables, being that guy throws off the social situation a bit (come on, don't kid yourself). Of course this is a pretty flimsy excuse to make a U-turn; and ultimately it has been quite easy to deal with.

I'm still happy with my decision.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

From moment to moment it can seem blindly obvious or completely unknowable what my culture is. As an english-speaking white guy in his forties that has a road-legal car and no debts, it's sometimes easy to focus on those aspects that others have quick access to, and lazily put myself in that big category.

A story I tell people is that since I grew up in the 80s in Newtown, Wellington, I didn't realise how particularly diverse my community was - and also how homogeneous other parts of Wellington were. Newtown has commonly been a place where immigrants and refugees can get accomodation (including the family on my mum's side), but as a kid I just took that all at face value. Being there surely contributed to my values and identity - I feel that I judge people by their actions and not by whichever culture they might appear to fit into.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

I can be a focussed investigator if something interesting strikes me as requiring some sudden and deep research. So, rather than creating endless rabbit-holes to disappear down, I can deploy this as a well-practiced strategy - rapid research and digestion to get just the information I need and move on.

I've never really stopped learning! I like learning something new every day and am able to take on new concepts and approaches quickly. I desire knowledge.

Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

Right now I've made myself a lot of room, and dropped a couple of things, to focus almost singularly on this learning journey. I don't feel particularly limited, but I am probably prone to being easily distracted if I'm not invested in what I'm doing.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

Keeping a commercial coffee machine clean is important (and not just clean on the outside). However, plenty of baristas have learned bad habits from others, or just don't get paid enough to care. This was a constant battle for me, as a head barista at a busy cafe.

There was a particular person that really didn't care to follow best practices. One evening I tried to challenge them properly about their habits, man-splaining the reason for a certain cleaning process, and rearranging the equipment to show them what I meant. Surely reason & practical application would win the day! Nope. They wasted no time in putting it back the way they liked it (a configuration that would, over time, damage the machine!).

OK. Now what? It was the end of the day so I was easily dragged to their level. I put it back my way. They put it back their way. Yeah now we're really getting nowhere together.

I kept it together, and had a deep breath. "I'm going to start getting pretty angry about this" I forecasted. The recommendation that came back was something in the vein of "well just don't look".

That person didn't care to update their habits. I think I had to leave it at that, and just ensure they weren't doing the daily clean. I'm happy that I kept it human, and didn't do or say anything horrible. So what might I try today? It might've been best to not engage from the start, and revisit the topic some other day, at the start of the day. Maybe I should've stuck to just using words, and not physically changing their setup, which probably just increased the likelihood of beligerence.
