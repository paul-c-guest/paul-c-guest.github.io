Problem Solving

My approach to navigating obstacles, what trips me up, and what goes well.

5 July 2023

A Simple Problem

So, this one time at code camp … I was sure I had written this fizzbuzz function to perform exactly as was required. However, the tests kept throwing up screeds of errors and red text. I didn't really read the messages, but rather just scrolled through quite quickly to get a flavour of how much errors were being thrown at me. "What the hell?" I pondered to myself.

I jumped back to my code, and combed through it again. Nope, still can't find a problem. I took a mental step back and noticed that the way I was running the tests meant that any other test with similar names in the title were also triggering!

Gah! It was as simple as that. The function that I was trying to test had been passing tests all along — and I had been too easily distracted by the amount of red herrings to notice the short success message in the first place. I had to laugh at myself.

From this I learned to pay attention to the bigger picture, to stop and step back for a wider view when things seem to be going wrong.

An Elegant Solution

I dropped myself in a deep end a few years ago. While still making baby-steps with Java, I put myself through an algorithms course. It was challenging from the very start! I vividly remember a particular first-week problem, and the moment when it all came together:

The problem was around a concept called 'percolation'; discovering whether a porous substance might hold a way through the structure from one side to the other (think: determining if an unexplored cave network will get you through the mountain). Our task was to write the code that would test for this capacity in any supplied model — and that it would be optimised to work as quickly as the tutors knew it could!

Silly me, I probably started by trying to write some code. I quickly went to pen & paper; drawing mazes and trying to understand how the tutors' code could solve each new case so quickly! The problem was too abstract to understand on paper, so I started talking about the problem with my partner (she was interested in what I was doing, but maybe not in this problem!). Little did I know I was rubber-ducking my way to a realisation.

The process made me feel like I might have bitten off more than I could chew; but it also sparked something in me — I had to crack this (and I knew that many others before me had solved this). It was definitely frustrating to come up against a tough mental challenge after a few years of slack brain activity.

The solution came to me while I was trying to go to sleep. The problem had certainly consumed my thoughts for a couple of days, and while I felt at the time that the solution was so strange that it wasn't elegant — the proof was in how quickly it could correctly process all the test cases. The code became shorter as well, and still has a eureka! comment above the section that brought it all together.

I learned that solving a problem with code was hardly about writing the code!

My Confidence In Each Technique

Pseudocode
 I've had a few years of practice and do this naturally now
Trying something
 Happy to just get stuck in with new ideas when appropriate and not making destructive changes
Rubber ducking
 I'm familiar with the idea and have done it a little, but only until my audience is in tears
Reading error messages
 Plenty of experience but sometimes I'm guilty of not bothering to going through a huge stack trace if there isn't a friendly error message to help, and I have my own inkling about where the likely problem is
Logging to console
 Happy with this technique, and use it a lot, but could certainly utilise more of the console methods to make output better to understand quickly
Googling
 Generally I find answers to my problems quickly, or realise I haven't worded my search well enough to get good results
Asking peers
 Lower confidence due to a combination of getting quick answers from google, and not thinking about aksing peers!
Asking coaches
 Similar to "asking peers", with the added layer of probably choosing to ask peers before coaches.
Reflection
 Solutions to problems that are mulling in the back of my head might suddenly come to the front of my head – however I don't think have some structured strategy to actively reflect about the problem I am trying to solve

Asking For Help

A time I was reluctant to ask for help: As part of a previous job, our team installed and used some bespoke techy hardware. I had a good relationship with the person that makes the gear, though felt like I was only ever asking for help or not solving my own problems before bothering them with our questions. So sometimes I limited the amount of contact I made with them, which was silly, and could have only meant things would take longer.

Next time I notice myself doing this, I'll just reach out instead of second-guessing how much contact is too much contact. After all — in this case there was literally no other person I could ask for help!
